The Shape of Social Capital in the United States

Code/Story: Diego Valdez Duran

Of all the factors that shape economic opportunity in America, social capital is one of the least visible yet most powerful. It’s about who you know, the networks you’re part of, and how those connections can open doors—or close them. And just like income inequality, social capital isn’t evenly distributed. In some counties, cross-class friendships and civic engagement are strong; in others, networks remain fragmented and insular.

This is what people mean when they say that “it's not what you know, it's who you know.”

But what does social capital look like across the United States? And how does it connect to economic mobility—the ability to climb the social and income ladder? Inspired by researchers at Meta, Harvard, NYU, and Stanford, we explored these questions using data from the Social Capital Atlas. This dataset, which maps social connections among Facebook users aged 25 to 44 living in the United States, gives us the opportunity to uncover insights across counties, ZIP codes, high schools, and colleges.

Instead of focusing solely on income or education, we wanted to understand how connections—both within and across socioeconomic groups—shape opportunity. Are people from different backgrounds forming meaningful relationships? Or are their networks reinforcing divides? And how do these patterns vary geographically?

To begin addressing these questions, we analyzed social capital metrics like economic connectedness, social cohesiveness, and civic engagement alongside measures of economic mobility.

Let’s begin with a macro view: a map of economic connectedness across U.S. counties as a lens into social networks shaping economic mobility. The color intensity reflects the prevalence of high-SES (socioeconomic status) friendships among low-SES individuals. Bright regions signify stronger integration across socioeconomic lines.

One striking pattern is the disparity in economic connectedness. Urban centers and regions with diverse economic opportunities tend to show higher scores, while rural areas and some economically isolated regions fall behind. This variation highlights uneven opportunities for forming cross-class friendships, a factor closely tied to upward mobility. Darker hues in the South and Southwest emphasize fragmented networks in these areas, shaped by systemic challenges like residential segregation and economic inequality.

In the final metric we’ll examine at the macro level, we explore friending bias as a measure of the gap between exposure and actual cross-class friendships. Interestingly, for low-SES individuals the Midwest reveals pockets of low or even negative bias, indicating areas where cross-class friendships exceed expectations based on exposure. However, much of the U.S. map falls within a relatively narrow range of friendly bias, from -0.02 to 0.16, distributed uniformly across various regions. This uniformity suggests a broad but modest variation in the extent to which shared spaces lead to meaningful social integration. A few counties stick out like Todd, SD, DeSoto, FL and Corson, SD as exhibiting particularly high bias.

The switch from low-SES to high-SES friending bias is drastic. High-SES individuals generally exhibit much lower bias, with most values falling below 0. In densely packed urban and suburban areas, bias tends to hover closer to 0, reflecting slightly more balanced opportunities for interaction within socioeconomic groups. However, regions in the South, particularly concentrated around Georgia, stand out with notably low bias, highlighting areas where high-SES individuals are more integrated into their communities, although within limited socioeconomic spheres.

Let’s compare that to the Bias High Own SES vs. Bias High Parent SES, where we see the pattern shifting significantly compared to previous visualizations. The majority of points are negative, and so for most colleges shown, high-SES students have lower economic connectedness within their SES group compared to what would be expected based on exposure. Thus, there’s a greater cross-SES integration for high-SES students overall, compared to a more neutral pattern for non-high-SES students. The outliers in the bottom left, mostly community colleges and technical schools, show institutions where high-SES networks are particularly weak relative to exposure. These schools often serve more diverse populations, possibly decreasing high-SES within-group connections. More generally, the slight negative trend along the diagonal suggests a relationship where higher bias in parental SES is associated with reduced bias in students' own SES. Larger schools cluster more tightly near the center, which makes sense as there’s less variation in SES-related bias. So, larger institutions may create environments that are more consistent in SES interaction making for less extremes of integration or isolation.

Building on the previous analysis of SES networks, let’s now focus on different metrics of social connections: clustering, support ratio, and volunteering rate. These metrics give us insight into the cohesiveness of social networks within groups and the role of civic engagement. Clustering measures the average fraction of an individual's friend pairs who are also friends with each other, indicating the closeness of social circles. Support ratio gives the proportion of within-county friendships where the pair of friends share a third mutual friend within the same county as local social density. Volunteering rate gives the percentage of individuals participating in activities related to volunteering or activism as a measure of community involvement. Counties with high clustering tend to be small, with populations below 15,000. In these areas, close-knit social networks are naturally stronger, supported by nearly 100% support ratios. Similarly, counties with high volunteering rates also show nearly 100% support ratios, suggesting that strong local connections may encourage civic participation and community engagement.

Let’s take a look at a different view, examining high schools ranked by the number of students (grade 9-12). We see that larger schools tend to have lower clustering (~30%) and very low volunteering rates, meaning that these social networks are less close and limited in terms of civic engagement. On the other hand, if we sort by high clustering values then we see that very small schools appear at the top (less than 100 students) where connections are more close and common. Interestingly, the highest volunteering rates also correlated with some of the higher clustering values, indicating that closer social networks may encourage greater civic engagement.

Finally, we focus on colleges ranked by their highest support ratios. These colleges typically have relatively small cohort sizes, averaging around 500 students, and have moderate clustering levels ranging from low (around 30%) to high (up to 70%). However, there’s no strong patterns that emerge. Though, there seems to be a relationship between high support ratios and volunteering rates as the highest volunteering rates tend to have high support ratios and moderate-to-high clustering. This highlights the relationships between social structures and community involvement in smaller institutions, which we’ve seen shared amongst other groups across the data.

Across the dataset, we've observed key trends linking social metrics to institutional and geographic characteristics. Smaller institutions and communities consistently showcase higher clustering and support ratios, indicating tightly-knit networks. Volunteering rates, while less consistent, tend to align with these cohesive structures, suggesting that strong local connections often encourage stronger civic participation. On the other hand, larger institutions and counties display less clustering and integration rates, likely due to their more diverse networks. These insights collectively highlight the role of community size and social integrations in defining both economic and civic mobilities.
